Strategic premise: A single buying signal is noise. A cluster of three or more correlated signals from the same account, firing within a 21-day window, is statistically indistinguishable from declared intent. This workflow operationalizes that distinction — detecting intent before accounts enter a formal evaluation process, and converting it into personalized outreach that earns a response.

Score and Filter Accounts

Hard filters run first. Accounts that pass are scored 0–100. Only accounts scoring 60+ reach Stage 3 copy generation.

Hard Disqualifiers (checked before scoring)

The following attributes disqualify an account regardless of signal strength. These are not subjective — they are binary filters:

  • Employee count < 200 — below this threshold, CS teams are too thin for a QuadSci deployment to be meaningful
  • Funding stage: Seed or Series A — budget doesn't exist; ARR is too low to justify
  • Estimated ARR < $50M — renewal economics don't create the urgency QuadSci addresses
  • No visible CS or RevOps function — no buyer exists; no one owns the renewal forecast
  • Consumer SaaS or non-B2B product — product telemetry patterns are fundamentally different
  • Vertical with 18+ month procurement cycles (federal government, healthcare payers) — deal velocity incompatible with signal TTL
  • Already a known QuadSci customer or active prospect in pipeline — checked against CRM before enrichment

Stage 3 Threshold

A score of 60+ (Tier 1 or Tier 2) triggers copy generation. Below 60, the account sits in Tier 3 (nurture) or Discard. The rationale: generating personalized AI copy has a non-zero cost (OpenRouter credits) and a non-zero risk (bad copy reaching a prospect and burning the account). We only spend copy-generation resources on accounts where firmographic and signal evidence is strong enough to justify a real outreach attempt.

Prompt Iteration — What Didn't Work and What I Changed

The exercise asks for prompt transparency. Here's the real iteration history:

❌ Iteration 1 — What Failed

Prompt: "Write a cold email for a VP RevOps selling QuadSci's revenue intelligence platform."

Output problem: Generic SaaS outreach. "Transform your revenue operations with AI-powered intelligence. Our platform helps companies like yours reduce churn and unlock growth..." — could have been sent by any vendor. Zero signal reference.

❌ Iteration 2 — Still Wrong

What I added: Passed the signal summaries. Added "reference the signals."

Output problem: Model led with the product pitch anyway — "QuadSci's Growth AI predicts churn 12 months in advance..." as the opening line. Signals were mentioned in the middle as an afterthought. The opener is everything in cold outbound; this didn't work.

❌ Iteration 3 — Better, Still Generic Opens

What I added: "Open with what you observed about this account."

Output problem: Model opened with "I recently came across your company and noticed some interesting signals..." — technically followed the instruction but kept using filler phrases. The first sentence was wasted.

✅ What Finally Worked — Three Specific Changes
  1. Added the explicit negative list: "Do not use: I hope this finds you well, I came across your company, reach out, circle back..." — naming the specific phrases the model defaulted to stopped the behavior immediately.
  2. Added structural rules in order: OPEN → CONNECT → BRIDGE → CLOSE, with each step defined. This gave the model a scaffolding it followed reliably.
  3. Locked the Clari quote verbatim in the system prompt: Without this, the model fabricated customer stories ("companies like Salesforce have seen..."). Including the real quote with attribution and telling the model to use it verbatim when relevant eliminated hallucination risk on proof points.

Stage the Campaign

How the workflow ends. What gets auto-sent, what gets held, and how to prevent a bad send from burning a high-value account.

Staging Mechanism

A Google Sheets tab called Review Queue serves as the human gate. n8n writes one row per Tier 1 account: generated email subject, body, buyer name, company, score, signals, and a Your Action dropdown (APPROVE / REJECT). The AE reviews the row, reads the email, and types APPROVE. n8n polls every 15 minutes and sends the email via Gmail on the next cycle.

TierMechanismHuman InvolvementSpeed
Tier 1 (80+) Google Sheets Review Queue → AE approval → Gmail send via n8n Required — AE must type APPROVE Same day (within 15-min polling cycle of approval)
Tier 2 (60–79) Apollo.io sequence enrollment — fully automated, no human review None — hands-off after setup Automatic on cluster trigger
Tier 3 (40–59) Logged in Scored Accounts tab. Status: Nurture. No email sent. None — monitored passively Re-triggered if new signal fires
Discard (<40) Logged and suppressed for 90 days None —

Confidence Threshold Logic

Two factors determine whether an account requires human review or goes straight through:

  • Score ≥ 80 → human gate required. High-score accounts are the most valuable and therefore carry the highest cost of a bad send. An AE who reviews the email catches factual errors, tone issues, or edge cases the AI missed. 20 minutes/day to review 1–3 accounts is a worthwhile tradeoff.
  • Score 60–79 → auto-route to Apollo sequence. These accounts are qualified enough to warrant outreach but not so valuable that a suboptimal email risks a critical relationship. The Apollo sequence is a standard 4-touch cadence — structured, not just a one-shot email — which provides natural correction if the first touch misses.
The AE can override at any time. If the Review Queue row shows a signal that's been misattributed, a buyer the AE already knows, or a company that should be suppressed — they type REJECT and the row is skipped. No email is sent, and the account status updates to "Rejected" in Scored Accounts. This is the safety valve.

Feedback Loop — How to Know If It's Working

The workflow is designed to self-improve over time. Three feedback mechanisms:

  1. Reply tracking: Gmail threads are monitored for replies. When a reply comes in, the AE logs the outcome (Meeting Booked / Not Interested / No Reply) back to the Review Queue row. Over 60 days, this data shows which signal combinations and which tiers generate responses — and the scoring rubric is adjusted accordingly.
  2. Signal-to-tier conversion rate: Of every cluster that fires, what percentage reaches Tier 1 or 2? If the rate is too low (<20%), the cluster definition is too loose — tighten the anchor signal requirement. If too high (>60%), the firmographic filters are too permissive.
  3. Send Date → Meeting Booked time: Track the average days between send and meeting booked. If meetings are coming primarily from accounts where multiple anchor signals fired, this validates the weighting in the scoring rubric.

Signal Prioritization — How Outcomes Feed Back Into the Model

The scoring rubric is not static. Signal weights are calibrated quarterly based on actual reply-rate and meeting-booked data. The feedback loop runs in one direction: outcome data → scoring weight adjustment → next quarter's routing decisions.

How it works in practice: After 60 days of sends, I'll have enough data to compare conversion rates by signal type. If accounts where Signal 2 (G2 review citing forecast miss) fired are booking meetings at 2–3× the rate of accounts where only Signal 1 (job posting) fired, Signal 2's weight increases from +15 to +20 and it gets reclassified from Anchor to Required Trigger — meaning no cluster forms without it. Conversely, if Signal 4 (leadership hire) consistently produces low-quality conversations, its weight drops or it gets demoted to a supporting multiplier rather than a standalone cluster contributor.

The specific calibration questions I'd answer at 60 days:

  • Which signal type, when present as the anchor, produces the highest reply rate? Increase that signal's weight by 5 points.
  • Are Tier 2 auto-sends generating replies at the same rate as Tier 1 approvals? If yes, relax the human gate to Tier 1 scores ≥ 90 only — the 60–89 band auto-sends without review.
  • What is the average days from signal fire to cluster trigger? If it's consistently >14 days, the 21-day window is too long and signal TTL should tighten to 14 days to focus on higher-recency intent.
  • Which accounts that scored Tier 3 (40–59) ended up booking meetings through other channels? If there's a pattern, the scoring rubric is under-weighting something — usually CS team size or tech stack fit.

⚠ Honest Assessment: The Biggest Risk

Signal noise producing false positives at scale. Google Alerts and LinkedIn RSS are keyword-match systems. They will surface articles that mention "Gainsight" in a positive context, G2 reviews that express frustration with a different product feature, and earnings calls from companies that are discussing churn as a competitor's problem — not their own. If the cluster detection doesn't filter these aggressively enough, the AI will generate and queue emails for accounts that are not actually in pain.

The mitigation in this design:

  • The anchor signal requirement (Signal 2 or Signal 5 mandatory) filters out the highest-noise signals from firing alone
  • The AE approval gate for Tier 1 catches false positives before they reach a buyer
  • Clay Claygent enrichment, which runs between Stage 1 and Stage 2, provides a second human-quality research pass that often catches misattributed signals

What I'd do with more time/budget: Add a pre-scoring AI classification step that reads each raw signal and rates it "High / Medium / Low conviction" before it counts toward a cluster. A G2 review mentioning "forecast inaccuracy" is High conviction. A Google Alert that picked up a Gainsight press release is Low conviction. This step would require a Claude API call per signal — ~$0.001/signal at current pricing — and would materially reduce false positive rate before it reaches the scoring layer.

System Architecture

Six n8n workflows running on localhost (self-hosted, $0). All importable as JSON. Credentials: Google Sheets OAuth2, OpenRouter HTTP Header Auth, Gmail OAuth2. (WF2 originally required Apify HTTP Header Auth; pivoted to Hacker News + Algolia API — no auth needed.)

WF1 — Signal Collector
Daily 7am
Polls 5 Google Alerts RSS feeds (job postings, G2, NRR/LinkedIn, leadership hires, earnings) + Reddit r/CustomerSuccess RSS. Parses XML → normalizes to Signal Log schema → appends to Google Sheets Signal Log tab. All 5 signal types covered.
WF2 — HN Buyer-Intent Collector
Weekly Monday 9am
Queries the Hacker News Algolia API (free, no-auth) for stories matching (Gainsight OR ChurnZero OR "customer success operations" OR "renewal intelligence" OR "churn prediction"). JS Code node filters hits against ICP keyword whitelist, extracts company domain from each post's link, and appends rows to Signal Log. Pivoted from the original Apify LinkedIn-jobs design (see Engineering Pivot note below). Architecture is unchanged; only the signal source swapped.
Engineering Pivot — WF2 signal source: WF2 was originally designed around LinkedIn jobs scraping via Apify. During build I determined that the canonical Apify LinkedIn-jobs actors are either non-existent in the official apify/ namespace, paid beyond the $0 budget, or fragile against LinkedIn's anti-bot measures. I pivoted to Hacker News + Algolia API as the signal source: free, no-auth, stable, and arguably higher buyer-intent — a founder posting "we're ripping out Gainsight" is a stronger qualified-account signal than a job posting that mentions Gainsight in passing. The workflow architecture is unchanged; only the signal source swapped. In production, this same WF2 pattern scales to multiple parallel signal sources (Reddit, G2 reviews, podcast transcripts, paid Apify actors when budget allows) all feeding the same Signal Log schema.
WF3 — Cluster Detector
Every 6 hours
Reads all Status=New rows from Signal Log. Groups by company domain. Checks: 3+ signals in 21-day window AND at least one anchor signal (type 2 or 5). New clusters are deduplicated against Scored Accounts. New accounts appended with status Pending Enrichment. Cluster data POSTed to Clay webhook for enrichment.
WF4 — AI Scorer
Webhook (triggered by Clay)
Receives Clay's enrichment-complete callback. Reads enriched account row from Scored Accounts. Calls OpenRouter (GPT-4o-mini, JSON mode) with full scoring rubric + account data. Parses structured score response (firmographic_score, signal_score, tech_score, total_score, tier, reasoning). Updates Scored Accounts row. Routes Tier 1/2 to WF5.
WF5 — Copy Generator
Webhook (triggered by WF4)
Receives scored account data. Calls OpenRouter with system prompt (QuadSci facts, rules, Clari proof) + dynamic user prompt (signal summaries, buyer, firmographics). Assembles Review Queue row. Appends to Review Queue tab. Updates account status to In Review Queue.
WF6 — Approval Monitor
Every 15 min
Polls Review Queue for rows where Your Action = APPROVE and Send Date is empty. Parses generated email into subject + body. Sends via Gmail (reply-to: AE email). Stamps Send Date. Updates Scored Accounts status to Sent.
Demo note on WF4 webhook: Clay (external service) cannot call a localhost webhook during a local demo. For the demo, WF4 is triggered manually in n8n after manually filling in fake enrichment data for the test account. In a production deployment on n8n Cloud or any VPS, Clay's on-completion webhook fires WF4 automatically — the architecture is correct, only the host changes.

Honest Limits and What I'd Do Differently

What doesn't work well, what this design can't do, and what budget or time would unlock.

Company Domain Attribution is Manual

Google Alerts and RSS feeds return article titles and URLs — not company domains. Matching a G2 review or a Reddit post to a specific company domain requires an enrichment step (Clay Claygent) or manual review. False attribution — matching a signal to the wrong company — is a real risk that produces emails sent to the wrong buyer. Fix with budget: A lightweight NER (named entity recognition) step before cluster grouping would extract company names from signal text and validate against a Crunchbase or Apollo domain database.

Buyer Identification is Semi-Automated

Without LinkedIn Sales Navigator or ZoomInfo, finding the VP RevOps email at a 300-person company requires: (1) Apollo free tier credits (50/month), (2) Clay Claygent AI research, or (3) manual LinkedIn search. At scale (>50 accounts/month), credits run out. Fix with budget: Apollo API ($49/month) or PDL person API gives programmatic email lookup. Alternatively, Apollo's free sequence enrollment accepts company domain + title and finds the contact automatically during enrollment.

Apollo Tier 2 Enrollment is Manual (Free Tier)

Apollo's free tier has no outbound API. Tier 2 auto-enrollment described in the architecture requires manually adding contacts in Apollo's web UI. In practice this means checking the Scored Accounts sheet daily for Tier 2 accounts and enrolling them by hand. Fix with budget: Apollo API ($49/month) or Outreach/Salesloft webhook enables true programmatic enrollment from n8n.

WF1 Signals Require Company Attribution to Cluster

Google Alerts RSS feeds return article titles and URLs — not company names or domains. WF1 writes empty Company Domain fields to Signal Log. WF3 skips rows with an empty domain, which means WF1-sourced signals never contribute to a cluster today. In production, a company attribution step must sit between WF1 and WF3 — an NLP pass that extracts the company name from the article title, then looks up its domain via Clay or Apollo. For the demo, the test data has proper domains. Fix with budget: A lightweight Claude API call ($0.001/signal) to extract company names from signal text, matched against a prebuilt ICP domain list, would solve this cleanly. WF2 (Apify) does partial domain extraction from LinkedIn company URLs — also imperfect but better than empty.

No RB2B Website Visitor Signal

The PRD included RB2B (free tier: 150 resolutions/month) as a 6th signal source — detecting when ICP contacts visit quadsci.ai. This would be Signal 0: the highest-conviction signal possible (they're already researching). RB2B's free tier pushes person-level LinkedIn profiles to an n8n webhook when a visitor matches an ICP profile. This was architecturally designed for but not yet implemented in the current version.

Copy Quality Degrades at Low Signal Quality

The email quality is directly proportional to the richness of the signal summaries fed to the AI. When signal summaries are thin ("job posting: RevOps role at Acme") rather than specific ("job posting: Senior RevOps Engineer — migrate off Gainsight, own renewal forecasting infrastructure"), the AI generates vaguer copy. The prompt rules constrain the output but can't create specificity that isn't in the input.

WF6 Sends One Email Per 15-Min Cycle

WF6's Code — Parse Subject + Body node uses $input.first() — it processes the first approved row in each cycle. If 3 accounts are approved simultaneously, WF6 will send only the first email and skip the other two until the next 15-minute poll, at which point they'll be picked up. For a typical daily workflow (1–3 approvals/day), this is acceptable. Fix: Replace the Code — Parse Subject + Body node with a Loop Over Items node to process each approved row in sequence within a single cycle.

No Slack Notification on Reply

The full data flow in the PRD includes an Apollo reply webhook → n8n → Slack notification loop. This closes the signal-to-outcome feedback loop automatically. It is not implemented in the current version because Apollo's free tier doesn't expose a reply webhook. Fix: Gmail label monitoring in n8n can detect replies to sent emails and post to Slack — no Apollo dependency needed.

What this design proves, even with its limits: A single GTM engineer with $0/month in hard tooling costs can run a signal detection → scoring → personalized copy → approval queue loop that would previously have required a 3-person SDR team and $2,000/month in data subscriptions. The limits above are real — but they're execution limits, not design limits. The architecture scales correctly with budget.
