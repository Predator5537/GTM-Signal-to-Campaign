Executive Summary

What I built: A signal-to-campaign workflow that monitors 6+ free signal sources, clusters them by account, runs deterministic scoring against QuadSci's ICP, generates persona-matched outbound copy (distinct prompt per tier), and stages a tiered review queue — all inside a single Clay table with one Apps Script send shim.

Why Clay over n8n: My first build was 6 n8n workflows. It worked, but enrichment was simulated manually — a fatal flaw for a workflow whose entire point is autonomous account research. v2 collapses 6 workflows into one Clay table with real waterfall enrichment, conditional AI execution, and tier-aware routing. Same architecture, dramatically less manual intervention, owned by a non-engineer GTM operator in production.

MVP scope baseline: This is an interview demo, not a production system. On-demand execution only (no recurring schedule), ≤10 qualified accounts, ≤20 enriched contacts, every send routed to my own inbox. The MVP exists to prove the technical and architectural competency. Production scope, paid tooling, and operational risks at scale are documented as future work in §9 and §10.

Routing in the MVP: Tier 1 generates email → human review in Clay → send on APPROVE (only tier that actually sends). Tier 2 and Tier 3 generate distinct-prompt drafts → write to the Clay watch-list → do NOT send. Discard is skipped entirely.

1. Strategic Thesis

QuadSci's product thesis is "telemetry over talk" — predict customer outcomes from raw product usage rather than CRM-derived guesswork, 12–18 months ahead of renewal, with 94% accuracy. The buyer who needs this most is a CRO at a 200–500-person post-Series B SaaS company who has been burned by a surprise renewal loss in the last two quarters and whose forecasting tooling didn't see it coming.

That buyer profile is the entire design constraint of this workflow. Every signal, every scoring weight, every line of email copy is reverse-engineered from one question: does this account give me evidence that this specific buyer is in pain right now?

Architectural one-liner: The whole pipeline is "match QuadSci's USPs to public buyer-pain signals, then write an email that proves you read the signal."

3. Stage 1 — Detect Signal Cluster

Each signal below is named, sourced from a free or open tool, and tied to a specific QuadSci pain. At least one is a direct anchor to QuadSci's USP of 12–18-month-ahead churn prediction.

3.1 The Five Signals I Monitor

Signal Why a CRO cares Source (free) Points (in scoring rubric)
S1 Job posting for CS platform migration or RevOps tooling A company hiring "RevOps Manager — Gainsight admin" or "CS Ops migration lead" is pre-announcing a 6–12 month evaluation cycle. This is the highest-volume, earliest signal. Greenhouse/Lever public boards via API; Google Alerts RSS for keyword combos 10 (single posting), +5 per additional
S2 G2 / Capterra review mentioning forecast miss or surprise churn (anchor) A buyer publicly writing "we had a renewal we didn't see coming" is articulating QuadSci's exact value prop. Highest-conviction signal in the stack. Google Alerts RSS scoped to site:g2.com with churn/forecast keywords 15
S3 CRO / VP CS LinkedIn post or comment on NRR / renewal blindness A leader publicly naming the pain is in active solution-seeking mode. Their post is also a citable opener for the email. Google Alerts RSS for site:linkedin.com "NRR" OR "renewal forecast" 15
S4 Director+ CS or RevOps hire announced A new CS/RevOps leader's first 90 days almost always include a tooling audit. Window of maximum receptivity to a vendor that can prove ROI fast. Google Alerts RSS for press releases; Reddit r/CustomerSuccess hire announcements 10

The USP-anchor signal (S2 + S5): A buyer publicly admitting they had a "renewal they didn't see coming" or an earnings call admitting NRR dropped — these are buyers describing the gap that QuadSci's 12-month-ahead, 94%-accurate churn prediction fills. The exercise asks for one signal tied directly to USP; I've built two, and they're both anchor signals (required for cluster qualification — see §3.2).

3.2 Cluster Definition

A cluster fires when an account exhibits ≥3 signals in the last 21 days, AND at least one of those signals is an anchor (S2 or S5). Both conditions must hold.

ConditionRationale
≥3 signals in 21 days Anything less is noise. A 21-day window is short enough that the buyer is still in the same head-space; longer windows let stale context slip in.
At least one anchor (S2 or S5) Job postings and LinkedIn posts alone can be misleading. Requiring a G2 review or earnings mention grounds the cluster in verifiable, public evidence of the specific pain QuadSci sells against. This is the single most important guardrail in the whole pipeline — it's what stops the workflow from chasing 50-person startups that happen to use Gainsight.
Domain dedupe Once a domain is in the Scored Accounts table, future cluster fires update the existing row rather than creating duplicates. Prevents the same VP CS getting two emails in two weeks.

4.4 Threshold for Stage 3 (Burning AI Credits on Copy)

The threshold to enter Stage 3 is Tier ≥ Tier 3 AND ICP hard filter passed. Discard rows never trigger the AI Column. This is enforced by Clay's per-row conditional execution — the AI Column has a condition tier != "Discard" and silently skips otherwise. At 100 Data Credits/month on free tier, this is the single most important capital protection in the design.

4.5 Buyer Identification (without paid tools)

For each qualified account, Clay runs in this order:

  1. Find Company — pulls domain, employees, funding, HQ. Native, ~2 credits.
  2. Find Person — searches for the right title at the right company. Search order tuned to QuadSci's primary-then-secondary buyer rule: (a) CRO → (b) VP Revenue Operations → (c) Chief Customer Officer → (d) VP Customer Success. First hit wins.
  3. Find Email — Clay's email waterfall (free tier providers: Hunter, Anymail, Snov). Verified emails only; unverified are flagged but not blocked.
  4. BuiltWith tech stack lookup for the Tech Stack Fit scoring component.

If Find Person returns zero results for all four title variants, the account is moved to a buyer_not_found status and the draft is not generated. This avoids generating perfectly-personalized copy for a nonexistent buyer.

5. Stage 3 — Generate Personalized Copy

5.1 The Prompts — Two Distinct Prompts for Two Distinct Tiers

Tier 1 and Tier 2 accounts have meaningfully different buyer states, so they get meaningfully different emails. Using the same prompt with a softer CTA would defeat the entire point of having a tier classification. Each tier runs its own AI Column in Clay, conditionally executed on tier == "Tier 1" or tier == "Tier 2". Tier 3 uses a third "nurture observation" variant. Model: claude-3-5-sonnet via Clay's native AI integration.

Why the tiers genuinely differ

TierWhat the score tells us about the buyerTherefore the email must
Tier 1 (80+) Strong firmographic AND credible anchor signal (earnings call / G2 review) AND tech stack fit. The buyer has visible, acute, board-level pain right now. They are actively in solution-seeking mode. Be direct. Reference the specific anchor signal by source. Assume the pain. Ask for a meeting this week. Tone: peer-to-peer "we both know what's happening."
Tier 2 (60–79) Strong fit but lower signal density, or strong signals with a softer firmographic/tech-stack pattern. The pain is likely present but not yet publicly acute. Buyer is in "noticing" mode, not solution-seeking mode. Be educational. Lead with a pattern observation, not a name-and-shame signal callout. Bridge to QuadSci as one data point. Offer a resource or a benchmark, not a meeting demand. Tone: "we see this pattern, here's how it usually plays out."

6.3 How to Prevent Bad Sends

Five guardrails, layered:

  1. Hard ICP filter before scoring — disqualified accounts never reach the AI Column.
  2. Anchor signal requirement — no anchor (S2 or S5) means no cluster, means no draft.
  3. Buyer-not-found halt — if Find Person returns zero, the row stops at buyer_not_found. No personalized email is generated for an unknown recipient.
  4. Tier 1 human review — explicit APPROVE required.
  5. Demo-mode send override — for this submission, every Gmail send is routed to my own inbox regardless of the actual buyer_email. The send code reads:
    const to = (CONFIG.demoMode) ? CONFIG.demoEmail : row.buyer_email;
    Toggle the boolean to flip from "safe for demo" to "live."

6.4 Feedback Loop — From Reply Rate Back to Signal Prioritization

The feedback loop has three tiers of data, each captured automatically into the same Clay table:

TierSignalHow capturedHow it feeds back
L0 — DeliveryDid the email bounce or land?Apps Script captures Gmail send response; updates delivery_statusHigh bounce rate on a given email-source provider → deprioritize that provider in the Find Email waterfall.
L1 — ReplyDid the buyer reply?Apps Script polls Gmail thread; updates reply_received + reply_sentiment (positive / neutral / negative / unsubscribe)Reply rate by tier validates the scoring rubric — if Tier 2 replies at 5% and Tier 1 at 6%, the tiering isn't doing real work and the rubric needs re-weighting.
L2 — MeetingWas a meeting booked?GTM operator logs manually in Clay column; alternatively Calendly webhook → ClayMeeting rate by signal type tells you which signals are pulling. If S5 (earnings) converts to meetings 3x more than S1 (job posting), reweight signal scoring +5 for S5 and –3 for S1 in the rubric.

The loop is closed by a monthly review where you sort accounts by outcome and look at: (a) which signal combinations produced meetings, (b) which prompt persona variant generated more replies, (c) which tier band is over- or under-converting. The scoring rubric weights are version-controlled in Clay's Formula Column — you change one number, the entire pipeline re-scores.

6.5 Honest Assessment — Biggest Risk and Mitigation

Biggest risk: The signal quality bottleneck. Every other piece of the pipeline (scoring, enrichment, copy, send) is downstream of how well I detect intent. Right now I'm detecting it from RSS-polled public sources — Google Alerts, Reddit, HN. These are lagging, noisy, and low-coverage. A G2 review takes weeks to appear after the actual surprise renewal. An earnings call lands quarterly. By the time my signal fires, a smart competitor already had the meeting.

What would actually fix this with budget:

  • Paid Apify actors (~$50/mo) for LinkedIn job + post scraping with same-day coverage — the highest-volume early signal.
  • Common Room or Default for capturing intent from Reddit/Slack communities in near-real-time.
  • RB2B (free tier 150 resolutions/mo) for person-level visitor identification on quadsci.ai — by far the highest-conviction signal possible (they're literally researching you right now), but requires a marketing website with traffic to be useful.
  • Crunchbase + PitchBook integration in Clay for funding-event signals (the day a Series C closes is the day the CS budget is fresh).

What I can mitigate today, free: Add more RSS feeds (15+ topic-specific queries vs. the current 5). Wire Reddit r/SaaS and r/RevOps in addition to r/CustomerSuccess. Scrape Greenhouse boards directly (no API needed, public HTML). Each addition is a marginal coverage win that doesn't change the architecture.

7.1 The Apps Script Send Shim (~30 lines)

// Runs every 5 minutes via Apps Script time trigger.
// MVP routing: ONLY Tier 1 with action="APPROVE" is eligible to send.
// Tier 2 and Tier 3 drafts are written to the watch-list and never sent.
// Production unlock for Tier 2 auto-send is gated behind a separate flag
// (CONFIG.allowTier2AutoSend) that requires ≥30 validated Tier 1 reply
// outcomes before it can be flipped — enforced in the query filter, not
// the script body.

const CONFIG = {
  demoMode: true,                              // ← flip to false for live sends (do not flip for the demo)
  demoEmail: "pmukhopadhyay5537@gmail.com",
  allowTier2AutoSend: false,                   // ← production flag, stays false in MVP
  clayTableId: "YOUR_CLAY_TABLE_ID",
  clayApiKey: PropertiesService.getScriptProperties().getProperty("CLAY_API_KEY"),
  signature: "\n\nBest,\nPrasun M.\nHead of GTM at QuadSci"
};

function sendQueuedEmails() {
  // MVP query: Tier 1 + APPROVE only. Tier 2 + Tier 3 explicitly excluded.
  const rows = fetchSendEligibleRows({
    tier: "Tier 1",
    action: "APPROVE",
    sendStatus: "queued"
  });

  rows.forEach(row => {
    const to = CONFIG.demoMode ? CONFIG.demoEmail : row.buyer_email;
    const body = (row.your_edits || row.email_body) + CONFIG.signature;
    try {
      GmailApp.sendEmail(to, row.email_subject, body);
      updateClayRow(row.id, { send_status: "sent", sent_at: new Date().toISOString() });
    } catch (e) {
      updateClayRow(row.id, { send_status: "failed", error: e.message });
    }
  });
}

Configured with a 5-minute time-driven trigger in Apps Script. Tier 2 and Tier 3 drafts are excluded from the send query entirely — they generate, populate the Clay table for review, and stay on the watch-list. Their send_status is set to not_sent_mvp_watchlist by the AI Column post-processing so the panel can see they were intentionally held, not failed.
